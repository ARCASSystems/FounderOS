The problem

You're running alone. Or close to it.

Every decision goes through you. Every client question. Every invoice. Every hire. Every piece of work that leaves the door.

You built it this way - not on purpose, but because you were the best person for every job when the business was small. And now the business grew around you. And now you can't leave.

Not for a week. Barely for a day.

This isn't a productivity problem. It's a context problem. The business runs because you hold everything in your head. Your standards. Your relationships. Your instincts about what to pursue. Nobody else has that. And because nobody else has it, nothing runs without you.

The tools haven't helped. You've tried Notion. Trello. Monday. Asana. The problem was never the tool. The problem is that every tool required you to maintain it. Another inbox. Another place to check. Another thing that goes stale when you get busy.

So you stop using it. And the cycle repeats.

Here's what's actually happening. Every time you open a new tool, you're creating a second brain. And a second brain competes with the first one. You spend more energy tracking two systems than either system is saving you.

What this is

Founder OS is an operating layer. Not a tool you open and close. A layer that lives where you already work - in Claude Code - and reads a small set of files every time you start a session.

Six files. That's the whole brain.

Who you are  ·  What you're focused on right now  ·  Open decisions  ·  Your active clients  ·  What's on for today  ·  What this week needs

identity.md

priorities.md

decisions.md

clients.md

daily‑anchors.md

weekly‑commit.md

From those six files, {{skill_count}} skills come alive. Skills that prep you for meetings, draft emails in your voice, log what happened, flag what you're avoiding, help you decide when you're stuck. You don't command them. You talk. They fire.

And once a week, the whole thing resets. You run the retro. You roll what's done. You park what's not. And you start Monday knowing exactly what matters.

The OS is yours, not automation. Every decision you log, every flag you track, every rant you capture - a human ran through it. The AI does the formatting. The judgment is yours.

Three parts

Hold it in your head as three things.

The Brain

Your six operating files. Identity. Priorities. Decisions. Clients. Daily anchors. Weekly commitments. What's true right now - not last quarter. Biased towards today, always. The brain needs feeding: three lines every time something real happens.

The Hands

{{skill_count}} skills. Write the email. Prep the brief. Draft the proposal. Log the decision. Post on LinkedIn. Each skill reads the brain and produces output that sounds like you. You don't memorize commands. You say what you need. The right skill fires.

The Heartbeat

Cadence. A daily anchor in the morning. A weekly retro on Sunday or Monday. Two moments where the system surfaces what's stale, ignored, or parked too long. Without the heartbeat, the brain goes stale and the hands stop firing clean output.

Where to start: tune three skills first

Forty-five skills is too many to set up cold. Pick three that match what you actually ship in the next seven days. Tune those. Ignore the rest until you need them. This is the difference between people who use the OS daily by week two and people who give up because they tried to perfect everything.

The language of AI

AI is trained on the world. That means it's trained on articles, books, code repositories, documentation, forum threads, emails. Almost all of it is structured text. And the most common structure across all of that text is Markdown.

Markdown is the language AI is best at reading. Headers, bullet points, bold text, numbered lists - these give the model clear signals about what matters, what's a heading, what's a list. When you write a wall of unstructured text, the model reads it like a wall. When you write with structure, it works with structure.

This is why every file in the OS is a .md file. Not because it looks nice in a text editor. Because Markdown is the format that gives the model the clearest signal about what matters.

The model is trained on the world. It doesn't know your world until you build the files. Every piece of context you add - a new client, a decision, a standard you hold - the model gets more specific. The output gets more useful. As the models improve, that context compounds. Same honest files, better model, better output. You don't start over when there's a new model. Your files stay.

You see how the AI reads your context as a network, not a folder of documents. It makes the structure tangible and helps you understand why adding one file in the right place can improve the output of six different skills at once.

Your files are the memory, not the session

Every AI session starts fresh. The model doesn't remember what you said last Tuesday. When you close the window, that conversation is gone.

This is why people get frustrated with AI assistants. They re-explain everything every time. They spend five minutes catching the AI up on what it forgot.

If you've tried AI assistants before and they didn't stick, here's exactly why. Every session started from scratch. The AI had no memory of your business, your standards, or your relationships. It wasn't the AI that failed. It was the missing context layer. That's what this OS provides.

Founder OS solves this by making context live in files, not in the session. The model reads the files every session. Fresh read, same truth. You don't re-explain. The files explain.

And because the context is in files, you're not locked to any one tool. Claude Code today, Codex tomorrow - they both read the same six files. The OS ships with an AGENTS.md file for exactly this reason. Non-Claude agents pick up the same operating rules.

Local-first means: the data lives on your machine. Not on a company's server. Not in someone's cloud. Plain markdown. Any text editor reads it. Any AI can load it. You own it.

Capture meetings before you forget them

Meetings are where decisions are made. Where commitments are given. Where relationships shift. But if you don't capture them, they might as well not have happened. You walk out with three important things in your head. By the next meeting you remember one.

With Granola
  • Meetings are transcribed automatically in the background
  • After the call, paste the summary into /capture-meeting
  • The OS pulls decisions, commitments, and client signals into the right files

  • Nothing slips through from a conversation you had weeks ago
Without it

Set Granola running before you start the call. Let it work in the background. Review the summary before you close the laptop. Then run /capture-meeting. That's the whole loop.

Rants

Humans don't think in bullet points. We think in rants.

A rant is an unstructured voice dump of what's in your head. No format. No cleanup. You don't need to know what you're trying to say before you say it. You just start talking.

The /rant command opens a capture. You dump. The system takes it raw and stores it in brain/rants/. Later, you run /dream. The dream skill reads the rant and distills it: patterns noticed, flags worth tracking, decisions you were circling, open questions.

Rant captures the mess. Dream gives it shape.

Most of your best thinking happens while you're walking, driving, or half-asleep. Not when you're sitting in front of a keyboard. Rants capture the real version of your thinking - not the cleaned-up one you'd type.
Rants are where you think. Prompts are where you ask. By the time you type a prompt, you should know what you're asking. The model is not there to do your thinking. The best prompts are the honest ones.

Talk to your system

If you're going to rant, use your voice. The friction of typing is exactly what kills the habit. When the gap between thought and capture is too big, you let thoughts go.

Talk to the system like you're thinking out loud. Then look at what came out. Edit it if you want. Paste it in. Speak, look back, refine. That process is how you get better prompts without spending 20 minutes composing a perfect question.

The best prompts are the honest ones.

Skills work in Cloud Claude too

Skills are markdown files. They are instructions Claude follows. That means they are not Claude Code-specific.

Claude.ai (web, desktop, mobile) reads skills natively. Tune your voice-interview, email-drafter, or proposal-writer skill in your local repo, then paste those same skill files into a Claude.ai project. The output is the same.

  • Drafting an email on your phone. Open Claude.ai mobile, paste in your voice profile and email-drafter skill, get the same output your laptop would.
  • In a meeting on a different machine. Cloud Claude with your skills pasted in matches what your local OS would produce.
  • Sharing one skill with a team member. They paste it into their Claude Project, no install required, same behaviour.

Skills are portable. Your repo is the source of truth, but the skills travel with you.

What stays local: hooks, scheduled tasks, file writes, and the session-start brief are Claude Code-only. Skills work everywhere. The rest of the OS runs on your machine.

Working with a team

If you have people around you, the OS adapts. But the setup changes.

The OS as described here is personal. The files hold your thinking, your relationships, your unfiltered decisions. That's not something you share wholesale.

What you share with a team is outputs, not brains.

When a skill produces a proposal, a meeting summary, an SOP, or a client update - that's a clean deliverable. It's ready to share. The OS has a pre-send check (/ship-deliverable) that runs before anything goes to a client. It verifies the output matches your voice, your standards, and the facts in your files.

For team members who want their own OS: they install separately. Same structure, different truth. Two operating layers that communicate through deliverables, not through shared files. Each person holds their own context. The outputs connect the work.

What not to put in

The OS is local. The files live on your machine. But they're also in git, and they sync to GitHub if you push. Know what that means.

Goes in

Priorities, clients, decisions, notes, flags, contact names, general business context. The brain should hold enough for the AI to be genuinely useful.

Stays out

Passwords. API keys. Bank details. Full government ID numbers. Anything a third party sent you in confidence. Legal advice you haven't reviewed. Anything you wouldn't want in a public document if the repo leaked.

The system has a privacy pre-commit hook. Anything you wrap in <private> tags gets stripped before it pushes to GitHub. Use it for anything borderline. But the cleaner practice is to not put sensitive data in the files at all.
On data: your files live on your machine. When you run a session, the OS includes them in your conversation context. That context is processed by your AI provider during the session. Check their privacy policy before adding sensitive business information to prompts.

The OS is a thinking layer, not a data warehouse. It should hold enough context to be useful and nothing that creates risk if it ends up somewhere unexpected.

When AI gets it wrong

It will. Plan for it.

The model is trained on the world. When it doesn't have enough context about your specific situation, it fills the gap with educated guesses. Confident ones. Sometimes wrong ones.

When output feels generic: the input was generic. Feed the brain more.

When output feels confidently wrong: push back. Ask the model to show its reasoning. A good model will revise with reasons. A bad one will just agree.

This is the trap. The model will agree with you if you push. It adapts to your framing. That's useful when refining output. It's dangerous when you want honest analysis. If you go in wanting validation, the system will validate you.

The forcing-questions skill exists for this reason. It asks six questions that have a real chance of killing the idea. That's the point. Better to kill a bad idea in six questions than spend three weeks building it with AI helping you go faster.

Don't ship AI output you haven't read. Every deliverable that leaves your machine has your name on it. The pre-send check (/ship-deliverable) is the built-in gate before anything goes to a client.

What can go wrong

Honest list. These are the failure modes.

You stop logging

The system goes stale. Stale context produces generic output. Generic output feels like AI, not like an assistant that knows you. You stop trusting it.

Fix   Three lines per day. Doesn't have to be more.

You fill everything in Week 1

You read the docs. You see {{skill_count}} skills and {{command_count}} commands and a whole brain layer to build. You try to set it all up before you use any of it.

Fix   Use before you optimise. Day 1 through 7. In order.

You forget the cadence

You skip the retro. Then again. Flags pile up. The system starts feeling like something you're behind on. You avoid opening it.

Fix   Put the weekly retro in your calendar. Sunday at 9pm or Monday at 8am. 20 minutes.

You treat it like a chatbot

You ask it questions without feeding it context. You expect generic AI output. You get generic AI output.

Fix   Quality in, quality out. Feed the brain. Use the skills.

You wait for perfect

You want every file filled perfectly before you run a real skill on a real piece of work. The bar keeps moving.

Fix   It will never be perfect. It will be yours. Run the skill. Use the output. Adjust.

The context window drifts

Long sessions on complex topics produce worse output at hour two than minute ten. You don't notice because you're in the flow.

Fix   Start a fresh session after two hours. The files hold context. The session doesn't need to.

The one thing

If you take nothing else from this playbook:

Log what happens.

Not in a diary. Not in paragraphs. Three lines in brain/log.md every time something real happens. A decision made. A meeting that shifted things. A deal that moved.

The system compounds from that. Skills get sharper. Stall detection gets accurate. Output starts sounding like something an assistant who's been with you for a year would say.

That's the whole game. Not the {{skill_count}} skills. Not the {{command_count}} commands. The log.
