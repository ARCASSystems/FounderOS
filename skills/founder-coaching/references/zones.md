# Zone Playbooks - Complete Reference

This file contains the full coaching playbook for each of the four operating zones. Read this when you need detailed guidance on how to approach a founder based on their current zone.

---

## Zone 1: Peacetime

The business is stable. Operational structure works. Revenue flows. Team delivers without the founder doing the work.

### What to Focus On

- Founder as Multiplier (3-10x) - strategy, vision, delegation, systems building
- Reducing Maker (0.1x) activities - anything the founder still does themselves
- Personal investment - health, learning, relationships, rest, hobbies
- Growth plays - new markets, new offers, new capabilities
- Building capacity for the team, not just the founder
- Succession planning - who steps up if the founder steps back?

### How to Approach

- Servant leadership mode - ask, don't direct
- Socratic questioning - help them think, don't give answers
- Long-term orientation - what happens in 6 months, 12 months, 3 years?
- Challenge comfort - peacetime breeds complacency
- Slower cadence - weekly or bi-weekly sessions are fine
- Bigger questions - "What do you actually want this business to become?"

### Questions to Ask in Peacetime

1. "What are you still doing that someone else could do 80% as well?"
2. "If you disappeared for a month, what would break?"
3. "What's the biggest opportunity you're not pursuing because you're too busy maintaining?"
4. "When was the last time you did something just for yourself?"
5. "What role are you playing because you enjoy it vs. because it's necessary?"
6. "What would your team say is the one thing you do that slows them down?"

### Watch For

- Founders who create artificial urgency because they don't know how to operate in peace
- Delegation avoidance disguised as "quality control" or "nobody does it as well as me"
- The hedonic treadmill - achieving goals but never feeling satisfied, always moving the target
- Creeping back into Maker mode because it feels productive
- Neglecting personal relationships and health because "things are going well at work"
- Boredom disguised as restlessness - some founders need crisis to feel alive

## Zone 2: Pre-War

Something big is coming. A major project, a new deal, an acquisition, a market shift, a seasonal peak, a regulatory change. The founder knows pressure is about to increase.

### What to Focus On

- Operational readiness - is the structure going to hold under pressure?
- Contingency planning - what breaks first? What's the backup plan?
- Role pre-assignment - who does what when the pressure hits?
- Energy reserves - is the founder rested and ready, or already depleted going in?
- Communication prep - does the team know what's coming?
- Financial runway - is there enough buffer to absorb the stress?

### How to Approach

- Planning mode - structured, specific, timeline-driven
- Stress test the plan - "What if X fails? What if Y is delayed? What if Z costs twice as much?"
- Pre-mortem - "Imagine the project failed. Why did it fail?"
- Doomsday Scenario - define the actual worst case to defuse irrational fear
- Slightly higher cadence - check in more frequently as the event approaches
- Practical, not philosophical - this is not the time for big-picture vision work

### Questions to Ask in Pre-War

1. "What's the single biggest risk, and what's the plan if it materializes?"
2. "If this goes badly, what's the worst realistic outcome? Can you survive it?"
3. "What's the minimum viable version of success? Not the ideal - the floor."
4. "Who on your team is going to feel the most pressure? Are they ready?"
5. "What can you delegate NOW before the pressure hits, so you have bandwidth?"
6. "Are you going into this rested or already running on fumes?"

### Watch For

- Founders who avoid preparation because it feels like "overthinking" or "negative thinking"
- Scope creep before the project even starts
- Emotional buildup disguised as strategic planning
- Over-preparing as procrastination - planning forever, never executing
- Not briefing the team because "I don't want to worry them"
- Underestimating the personal cost of what's coming

## Zone 3: Wartime

Active crisis. Market pressure. Team gaps. Revenue threats. The operational structure is not holding.

### What to Focus On

- Founder shifts from Multiplier to hands-on operator - this is necessary, not a failure
- Business continuity - what keeps the lights on right now?
- Triage - what are the BIG leaks? Fix those. Ignore everything else.
- Recovery path - how do we get out of wartime? What needs to be true?
- Sustenance - protecting core revenue, core team, core relationships
- Cash management - how long can we operate at this level?

### How to Approach

- Directive, not servant leader - clear instructions, fast decisions
- Forget agile niceties - three tasks max per sprint doesn't apply in a crisis
- Shorter cycles - daily or twice-weekly check-ins, not weekly
- Brutal prioritization - "What MUST happen today? This week?"
- Action over analysis - decisions with 60% information are better than no decisions
- Emotional regulation - the founder's energy sets the team's tone

### Questions to Ask in Wartime

1. "What's the one thing that, if it breaks, kills the business? Protect that first."
2. "What can you stop doing immediately that won't matter in 30 days?"
3. "Who do you need in the room with you right now? Get them there."
4. "What decision have you been putting off that would free up the most capacity?"
5. "What's your cash runway at current burn? How long can you sustain this?"
6. "Are you sleeping? Are you eating? When did you last take a full day off?"

### Watch For

- Founders who freeze instead of act - analysis paralysis under pressure
- Founders who refuse to get hands dirty because "that's not my role anymore"
- Heroic overwork without triage - being busy across everything instead of focused on leaks
- Team morale collapse - the founder's panic becomes the team's panic
- Isolation - founder stops talking to advisors, partners, team
- Denial - "It's fine, we just need to push through" when the data says otherwise

### Context-Dependent Triggers

- Market conditions: war in region, economic downturn, industry saturation, regulatory changes
- Internal crisis: key person leaves, cash flow crunch, major client loss, co-founder conflict
- Operational failure: systems breaking, quality collapse, delivery failures
- Team size multiplier: small teams (under 10) feel wartime impact 3-5x harder than larger orgs
- Industry matters: events industry has seasonal wartime; consulting has client-loss wartime; retail has supply-chain wartime

## Zone 4: Recovery

The crisis passed. Revenue is returning or stable. The immediate fires are out. But the founder and team are depleted.

### What to Focus On

- Rest is not optional - the founder needs to recover before building again
- Debrief - what broke, what held, what do we change for next time?
- System repairs - fix what wartime revealed as structurally weak
- Gradual transition back to Multiplier mode - don't rush it
- Celebrate wins - founders rarely acknowledge what they survived
- Team acknowledgment - the people who stayed through the crisis need recognition
- Rebuild personal reserves - relationships, health, hobbies that got sacrificed

### How to Approach

- Patient, not urgent - resist the temptation to "make up for lost time"
- Reflective - "What did we learn? What surprised us?"
- Forward-looking - "How do we make sure we're better prepared next time?"
- Honest about cost - "What did wartime take from you personally?"
- Longer sessions, less frequent - depth over speed
- Permission to be human - founders often feel guilty about needing recovery

### Questions to Ask in Recovery

1. "What did you learn about yourself during that crisis?"
2. "What broke that you expected to hold? What held that surprised you?"
3. "What relationship or commitment did you sacrifice that you need to repair?"
4. "If the same crisis happened again in 6 months, what would you do differently?"
5. "What do you need personally before you can lead at full capacity again?"
6. "What was the cost to your team? Have you acknowledged it?"

### Watch For

- Founders who skip recovery and jump straight to the next big thing
- Post-crisis depression disguised as "just being tired" or "feeling flat"
- Loss of confidence from things that went wrong during wartime
- Team attrition after the crisis passes - people often leave when the pressure lifts, not during
- Rebuilding too fast - the structure needs to be repaired before you load it again
- Pretending the crisis didn't affect them - founders who won't admit vulnerability

## Zone Transitions

Zones don't change overnight. Watch for these transition signals:

**Peacetime -> Pre-War:**
- New large contract or project on the horizon
- Market indicators shifting (competitor moves, regulatory changes, economic signals)
- Seasonal patterns the founder has seen before
- Team changes that reduce capacity

**Pre-War -> Wartime:**
- The plan isn't holding
- Revenue drops below sustainability threshold
- Key person leaves at the worst time
- External shock (market crash, pandemic, regional conflict)

**Wartime -> Recovery:**
- The immediate threat is contained (not necessarily resolved, but contained)
- Revenue stabilizes even if not growing
- The founder can sleep through the night again
- The team stops operating in crisis mode

**Recovery -> Peacetime:**
- Systems rebuilt stronger than before the crisis
- Founder back to Multiplier mode on most activities
- Energy scores consistently 3+ across all dimensions
- Forward planning replaces reactive decision-making
