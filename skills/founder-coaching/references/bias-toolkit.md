# Bias Challenge Toolkit - Complete Reference

This file contains the extended set of cognitive biases and behavioral patterns commonly found in founders, along with multiple challenge questions and guidance for surfacing them in coaching conversations.

---

## How to Use This Toolkit

1. Scan for patterns during the State Check-In and conversation
2. Don't lead with the bias name - founders don't need a psychology lecture
3. Use their own words as mirrors - "Last time you said X, today you're saying Y"
4. One bias per session is usually enough - don't stack challenges
5. The goal is awareness, not agreement. Plant the seed.

---

## Bias 1: Busy vs. Productive

**What it looks like:** The founder works 14-hour days. Calendar is packed with meetings. Always "in the middle of something." But when you ask what moved the needle this week, they struggle to name anything concrete. Activity feels like progress.

**Why founders fall into it:** Doing things feels safer than deciding things. Meetings feel productive. A full calendar validates the hustle. It's easier to respond to the urgent than to create the important.

**Challenge questions:**
- "Name three things that produced a measurable result this week. Not things you did - results."
- "How many hours this week were deep work vs. reactive work?"
- "If you could only work 4 hours this week, what would you do?"
- "Show me your calendar. How much of this was scheduled by you vs. scheduled at you?"

**Zone context:** This is mostly a peacetime and recovery problem. In wartime, being busy on the right things is correct. The issue is when peacetime busyness substitutes for strategic work.

## Bias 2: Excessive Self-Regard Tendency (Personal MBA)

**What it looks like:** The founder overestimates their ability in areas where they have limited experience. The less they know about a domain, the more confident they sound. Classic Dunning-Kruger. They give opinions on marketing, finance, or tech as if they're experts when they've never done it professionally.

**Why founders fall into it:** Building a company requires confidence. That confidence bleeds into domains where it isn't earned. Early success reinforces the belief that they can figure anything out. Nobody around them pushes back.

**Challenge questions:**
- "On a scale of 1-10, how competent are you at [X]? When did someone who does [X] for a living last give you feedback?"
- "What's the last time you were genuinely wrong about something important? What did it cost?"
- "Who on your team or in your network knows more about this than you? Have you asked them?"
- "If a candidate with your level of experience in [X] applied for a job doing [X], would you hire them?"

**Zone context:** Dangerous in all zones. In wartime, it leads to wrong decisions made confidently. In peacetime, it prevents hiring experts because "I can do it myself."

## Bias 3: Confirmation Bias

**What it looks like:** The founder seeks information that supports their existing plan. They read articles that agree with their strategy. They dismiss customer feedback that contradicts their vision. They surround themselves with people who validate their direction.

**Why founders fall into it:** They've invested identity and resources into the current path. Contradicting evidence feels like a threat to everything they've built. It's psychologically uncomfortable to consider being wrong.

**Challenge questions:**
- "What's the strongest argument against what you're doing right now?"
- "If a competitor was watching you, what would they exploit?"
- "What data would convince you you're wrong? Do you have that data?"
- "Who in your life tells you things you don't want to hear? When did you last talk to them?"

**Zone context:** Most dangerous in pre-war and peacetime when strategic decisions are being made. Less relevant in wartime where reality forces itself on you.

## Bias 4: Hindsight Bias

**What it looks like:** "I knew that was going to happen." After something goes wrong (or right), the founder reconstructs the past to feel like they predicted it. Creates a false sense of pattern recognition and control.

**Why founders fall into it:** It's comforting to believe we understand cause and effect. Admitting you didn't see it coming feels like incompetence. It also prevents learning - if you "knew," there's nothing to learn.

**Challenge questions:**
- "If you knew, what did you do about it at the time?"
- "What did you actually predict before it happened? Show me the note, the message, the plan."
- "Is it possible you're connecting dots in reverse?"
- "What's the lesson here if you admit you didn't see it coming?"

**Zone context:** Shows up most in recovery and debrief sessions. Prevents honest post-mortems.

## Bias 5: Akrasia (Personal MBA)

**What it looks like:** The founder knows what they should do but doesn't do it. "I should fix the pricing." "I should have that conversation." "I should start exercising." The "should" persists across sessions but never converts to action.

**Sources of akrasia (from Personal MBA):**
1. Can't define what they actually want
2. Task feels like it leads to something undesirable
3. Can't see the path from here to there
4. The goal feels so big it seems unachievable (triggers loss aversion)
5. The "should" was someone else's expectation, not the founder's own desire
6. Immediate gratification from other activities wins over long-term reward
7. Benefits are abstract and distant; other actions offer concrete immediate benefits

**Challenge questions:**
- "You've said 'I should do this' for three sessions now. What's actually stopping you?"
- "Let's apply the Five-Fold Why. Why do you think you should do this? Keep going deeper."
- "Whose voice is that 'should'? Is it yours, or someone else's expectation?"
- "What would happen if you just decided NOT to do it? Would anything actually break?"
- "What's the smallest possible version of this action you could take in the next 24 hours?"

**Zone context:** Universal. Shows up in every zone. In wartime, akrasia on critical tasks is dangerous. In peacetime, it's the primary reason important-but-not-urgent work doesn't happen.

## Bias 6: Avoidance Disguised as Strategy

**What it looks like:** The founder avoids the hard task - the difficult conversation, the sales call, the financial review, the firing decision. But instead of admitting avoidance, they fill time with easier tasks they label "strategic." Redesigning the website instead of calling the angry client. "Refining the deck" instead of having the pricing conversation.

**Why founders fall into it:** Hard tasks carry emotional risk - rejection, conflict, failure. Easy tasks feel productive and provide immediate satisfaction. The brain is wired to avoid discomfort.

**Challenge questions:**
- "What's the one thing you've been actively avoiding? Not what you haven't gotten to - what you don't want to do."
- "If I looked at your week, what would I see that was filler disguised as work?"
- "What would happen if you did the thing you're avoiding first thing tomorrow morning?"
- "What's the cost of each day you don't do this?"

**Zone context:** Peacetime and recovery especially. In wartime, avoidance usually looks like working on secondary fires while ignoring the main one.

## Bias 7: Founder as Cost Center

**What it looks like:** The founder puts in sweat equity without accounting for the actual cost of their time. 60-hour weeks are "free" because they're not paying themselves. They don't calculate the opportunity cost of their time. They also don't see the impact their pace has on co-founders and team members.

**Why founders fall into it:** "It's my company, so my time doesn't cost anything." The hustle narrative celebrates free labor. Accounting for your own time feels like an unnecessary overhead when you're bootstrapping.

**Challenge questions:**
- "If you paid someone your hourly rate to do what you did this week, would that be a good investment?"
- "What's the hourly cost of your time? Have you calculated it?"
- "What could you have done with those hours that would have been worth more to the business?"
- "How is your pace affecting the people around you? Not just the work - their energy, their expectations, their wellbeing."

**Zone context:** Universal, but most hidden in wartime when "everyone needs to sacrifice." In peacetime, it's the difference between a growing business and a glorified job.

## Bias 8: Power Misuse

**What it looks like:** Two forms. Under-use: the founder has authority but won't make tough calls. Avoids firing, avoids restructuring, avoids confrontation with partners or clients. Over-use: the founder micromanages, controls every decision, doesn't trust the team to execute without oversight.

**Why founders fall into it:** Under-use often comes from wanting to be liked or from conflict avoidance. Over-use comes from "nobody does it as well as me" or from anxiety about losing control.

**Challenge questions:**
- "What decision are you avoiding that only you can make?"
- "What are you controlling that someone else should own?"
- "If you gave [team member] full authority on this, what's the worst that happens?"
- "Are you using your power to move the business forward, or to make yourself feel safe?"
- "What would your team say is the one decision you should have made already?"

**Zone context:** Power under-use is dangerous in wartime (someone needs to make the call). Power over-use is damaging in peacetime (kills team growth and autonomy).

## Bias 9: Parkinson's Law (Personal MBA)

**What it looks like:** Work expands to fill the time available. A task that should take 30 minutes takes 3 hours because no constraint was set. Meetings scheduled for an hour use the full hour even when the topic is resolved in 15 minutes.

**Why founders fall into it:** No time pressure means no forcing function. Creative work especially tends to expand infinitely. Perfecting a 3 instead of shipping a 7.

**Challenge questions:**
- "What if you had to finish this in half the time? What would you cut?"
- "What's the minimum viable version of this task?"
- "Is this taking this long because it needs to, or because you let it?"
- "Set a timer for 25 minutes. What can you get done?"

**Zone context:** Peacetime problem primarily. Wartime naturally imposes time constraints.

---

## Bias 10: Malinvestment (Personal MBA)

**What it looks like:** Time, energy, or money invested in the wrong place. Not a bad investment - an investment in something that doesn't serve the actual goal. The sunk cost fallacy makes it hard to walk away because "we've already put so much into this."

**Why founders fall into it:** Sunk cost is psychologically powerful. Walking away feels like admitting failure. The original reasoning seemed sound. Nobody wants to write off effort.

**Challenge questions:**
- "If you started fresh today with everything you know now, would you invest in this again?"
- "What's the ongoing cost of continuing vs. the one-time cost of stopping?"
- "Is this serving the business or serving your ego?"
- "What could you do with the time and money you'd free up by killing this?"

**Zone context:** Important in all zones. In wartime, spotting malinvestment fast is survival. In peacetime, it's the difference between growth and stagnation.

---

## Bias 11: Locus of Control Confusion (Personal MBA)

**What it looks like:** The founder worries about things they can't control (economy, competitors, market sentiment, what people think of them) while neglecting things they can control (their product, their team, their pricing, their daily actions).

**Why founders fall into it:** External factors feel important and urgent. News cycles create anxiety. It's easier to worry about the market than to fix the pricing page. Worrying about uncontrollable things provides a reason not to act.

**Challenge questions:**
- "Of everything you just told me you're worried about - what can you actually influence?"
- "What have you done this week that was entirely within your control?"
- "If you stopped watching the news for a month, what would actually change in your business?"
- "Are you using external factors as a reason not to act on internal ones?"

**Zone context:** Extremely relevant in wartime where external factors are real (market crash, conflict, pandemic) but the founder still needs to focus on what they can control.

---

## Bias 12: Limiting Beliefs (Personal MBA)

**What it looks like:** A belief the founder holds that restricts what they think is possible. Often invisible to them because it's been there so long it feels like fact. "I can't charge that much." "That kind of client wouldn't work with someone like me." "I'm not a salesperson." "I need more experience before I can do that."

**Why founders fall into it:** Limiting beliefs often formed from early career experiences, cultural norms, family expectations, or a single bad experience that got generalized.

**Challenge questions:**
- "Says who?"
- "Is that a fact or a belief? What evidence do you have?"
- "Do you know anyone who has done exactly what you're saying is impossible?"
- "Where did this belief come from? Is it still true?"
- "What would you attempt if you knew you couldn't fail?"

**Zone context:** Shows up in peacetime strategic decisions and pre-war planning. Less visible in wartime when survival overrides beliefs.

## Challenge Principles

1. **One at a time.** Don't stack multiple bias challenges in a single conversation. Surface one, let it land, move on.
2. **Use their words.** The most powerful mirror is quoting what they said in a previous session that contradicts what they're saying now.
3. **Diagnose, don't attack.** "I notice a pattern" not "you're being biased."
4. **Let silence do the work.** After a hard question, wait. The discomfort is where the growth happens.
5. **Name the deflection.** If they change the subject, dodge the question, or make a joke - say so. "You just deflected. What was uncomfortable?"
6. **Follow up next session.** If a bias was surfaced but not resolved, bring it back. "Last time we talked about X. What's changed?"
7. **Know when to back off.** If the founder is in genuine crisis (wartime), bias challenges can wait. Triage first, coaching later.
